..... City Level Inequities
Although cities are heterogeneous in terms of residents and neighborhoods, they also have city-level characteristics that may affect health. City level characteristics that may related to differences in health (and COVID-19 outcomes) across cities include city poverty rates, income inequality, population density, race and ethnic composition and city-level segregation. Data
One of the main goals of this data platform is bring together COVID-19 Equity indicators across the 30 BCHC cities. We are using a combination of data sources, from data provided directly from health departments to publicly available data. Thank you to all of those health department employees who have help contribute to this effort. This section will give an overview of our data sources and track progress of our data collection efforts. In some cases (top table below) we use data from the cities themselves; in other cases (bottom table below) we use county proxies. As city-specific data becomes more available, it may replace country proxies.

Data Sources for Cities

Consistent with our equity framework, we collected data at three levels: 1) Individual-level (Race/ethnicity), 2) Neighborhood level (zip code tabulation area, or ZCTA), and 3) City-level. A ✔️ indicates that we have some data at this data level for this city. A ✖ is used to indicate cases where a certain data type was not available for the given city. Note that for Long Beach, available for the given city. Note that for Long Beach, ✖+ indicates data from Long Beach (city) is not available, and that Long Beach (city) is included in Los Angeles (County) data.

These data are obtained from many sources, including various city, county, and state health departments, the CDC, as well as Johns Hopkins University. You can click on each row for these details including:
  • Data Level: 1) Individual-level (Race/ethnicity), 2) Neighborhood level (ZCTA), or 3) City-level.
  • Data Type: Specific data type including COVID-19 outcomes (testing, incidence, mortality, hospitalizations) or vaccination outcomes (uptake and hesitancy).
  • Age-adjusted: Indicates if data is crude or age-adjusted (using the 2000 US Standard Population).
  • Source: A hyperlink to where the data was collected from. These sources include the following
    • LHD: Local health departments.
    • State: State health departments.
    • JHU: Johns Hopkins University data provided for the county or counties used as a proxy for the city.
    • CDC: A hyperlink to where the data was collected from. Data corresponds to the county or counties used as a proxy for the city. These sources include the following
      • Outcome Data comes from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, COVID-19 Response. COVID-19 Case Surveillance Data Access, Summary, and Limitations (version date: 2021-05-22).
      • Vaccine data comes from the CDC COVID Data Tracker.
      • The CDC does not take responsibility for the scientific validity or accuracy of methodology, results, statistical analyses, or conclusions presented.
  • Date of Access: Date that the county/city data source was accessed. For example, a value of 04/01/21 indicates that the data presented in this row consists of data from the start of the epidemic to 04/01/21. Note- there are often reporting lags, so this value may lag by a number of days or weeks depending on the county/city data source.
  • Geographic level: This column indicates whether the data collected was at the county or city level. Note, all consolidated city-county (Baltimore, New York City, Philadelphia, San Francisco, Washington, Denver) have been labeled as city.
Footnote: Many visualizations used in this dashboard include city names either in the axis or tooltips. A city name without an asterisk (i.e. Philadelphia) indicates that the source of data plotted in that visualization was categorized as City. A city name with a single asterisks (i.e. Los Angeles*) indicates that the source of data plotted in that visualization was categorized as County or CDC. A city name with a two asterisks (i.e. Indianapolis**) indicates that the source of data plotted in that visualization was categorized as JHU.

Therefore, it is necessary to use county-level data as a proxy for some cities. About
This dashboard was developed and is maintained by the Drexel Urban Health Collaborative in partnership with the Big Cities Health Coalition, with support from the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation and the De Beaumont Foundation. Any issues or bugs can be reported at our GitHub Repository.

The Drexel Urban Health Collaborative:

The mission of the Drexel Urban Health Collaborative at the Dornsife School of Public Health is to improve health in cities by increasing scientific knowledge and public awareness of urban health challenges and opportunities, and by identifying and promoting actions and policies that improve population health and reduce health inequities. Through three areas of emphasis, research and data, training, and community and policy engagement, the UHC works to advance knowledge, build capacity, and translate knowledge into community and policy actions to improve urban health in Philadelphia and in cities all over the world.

The Big Cities Health Coalition:

The Big Cities Health Coalition (BCHC) is a forum for the leaders of America's largest metropolitan health departments to exchange strategies and jointly address issues to promote and protect the health and safety of their residents. Collectively, BCHC member jurisdictions directly impact nearly 62 million people, or one in five Americans.

The Robert Wood Johnson Foundation:

The Robert Wood Johnson Foundation (RWJF), located in Princeton, N.J., is the largest philanthropy in America dedicated solely to health. Since 1972, we have been providing funding, assistance, and research for projects and programs to help people, their families, and their communities be as healthy as possible. RWJF is committed to working alongside others to build a Culture of Health that provides everyone in America a fair and just opportunity for health and well-being.

De Beaumont Foundation:

The de Beaumont Foundation advances policy, builds partnerships, and strengthens public health to create communities where people can achieve their best possible health. The Foundation's programs and investments strengthen the public health system, facilitate collaboration, and provide practical tools to improve the health of all Americans.

Castlight Health:

As a leader in healthcare navigation, Castlight Health provides a world-class digital platform with a team of clinical and benefits experts to help members easily connect and engage with the right programs and care, at the right time. Castlight partners with Fortune 500 companies and health plans to transform employee and member benefits into one comprehensive health and wellbeing experience to deliver better health outcomes and maximize returns on healthcare investments.

We use Castlight data on the location and characteristics of COVID-19 testing locations to assess inequities in accessibility to testing sites in BCHC cities.
